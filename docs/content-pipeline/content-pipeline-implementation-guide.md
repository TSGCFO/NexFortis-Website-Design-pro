## Part 1: System Architecture Overview

### 1.1 The Big Picture

The NexFortis content pipeline is a structured, semi-automated system that takes a topic idea through research, drafting, editorial review, publication, and distribution — all with minimal manual effort from Hassan. At a high level, it looks like this:

**Frase.io** researches the topic and produces an SEO-optimized draft. Hassan's unique knowledge is captured first through a **Telegram bot** that asks smart interview questions and stores his answers as input context for Frase. Once a draft is ready, it moves into **Sanity CMS** where Hassan reviews and approves it. When published, the **Next.js website** renders the article to readers. Simultaneously, a social scheduling tool (**Missinglettr** or **SocialBee**) detects the new article via RSS feed and begins distributing formatted posts across LinkedIn, X, Facebook, and Instagram over a 12-month drip schedule. A week or two after publication, the article is manually imported to **Medium** for cross-posting with a canonical link pointing back to the original.

### 1.2 Component Responsibilities

| Component | What It Does |
|---|---|
| **Telegram Bot** | Sends Hassan topic-specific interview questions weekly; collects text or voice answers; stores answers for Frase input |
| **Frase.io** | Conducts SERP research for each target keyword; generates outlines; drafts full articles; provides SEO content scoring; exports finalized drafts via API |
| **Sanity CMS** | Stores all article content, author profiles, and categories; provides a web-based editorial dashboard; manages publication status; serves content to the Next.js app via API |
| **Next.js (NexFortis site)** | Fetches blog content from Sanity at build time and on a revalidation schedule; renders article pages with SEO metadata, structured data, and Open Graph tags; exposes an RSS feed |
| **Missinglettr / SocialBee** | Reads the blog RSS feed; detects new articles; creates platform-specific social posts automatically; schedules a 12-month drip campaign across all four social accounts |
| **Medium** | Receives cross-posted articles via the Import Tool; canonical tag automatically points back to nexfortis.com/blog/[slug] |
| **Google Search Console** | Tracks keyword rankings, impressions, and click-through rates for all published articles |
| **Google Analytics** | Tracks organic traffic, time on page, and bounce rate per article |

### 1.3 Data Flow — Step by Step

The following describes the complete journey of a single article, from idea to live distribution:

**Step 1 — Topic Selection**  
Hassan identifies a target keyword/topic, typically aligned with one of three content pillars: QuickBooks, Managed IT & Microsoft 365, or Cybersecurity. This topic is entered into Frase and also queued for the Telegram bot.

**Step 2 — Interview Questions Sent**  
On the scheduled weekly send day (Monday morning), the Telegram bot fires a message to Hassan containing the topic name and 3–5 custom questions. These questions are generated by analyzing top-ranking content for that keyword and identifying the knowledge gaps that existing articles do not cover. Hassan's real-world experience fills those gaps.

**Step 3 — Answers Collected**  
Hassan replies in Telegram with text responses, or sends voice notes which are transcribed automatically. All answers are stored — attached to that specific topic — and passed to Frase as contextual input for the drafting step.

**Step 4 — Frase Drafting and Optimization**  
Within Frase, the article is drafted using Hassan's interview answers as the source of original insights. The Frase content score is pushed toward 80+ out of 100 by covering all the topics and entities found in top-ranking competitor articles. Once the draft is finalized in Frase, the Frase API pushes the article content, SEO score, title, and meta fields into Sanity with a status of "draft."

**Step 5 — Sanity Editorial Review**  
Hassan logs into the Sanity Studio dashboard. He sees the draft article, reads it, makes any manual edits, fills in any remaining fields (featured image, category, pillar tag, internal links), and runs through the quality checklist. When satisfied, he changes the status from "draft" to "published." Sanity records the publication timestamp automatically.

**Step 6 — Next.js Renders the Article**  
The Next.js site is configured to check Sanity for new or updated published articles on a revalidation interval (ISR). When the status changes to "published," the Next.js app will regenerate that page's static version within the revalidation window. The article page goes live at nexfortis.com/blog/[slug] with full SEO metadata, JSON-LD structured data, and Open Graph tags.

**Step 7 — Social Distribution Begins**  
The blog's RSS feed is updated automatically when the new article is published. Missinglettr (or SocialBee) polls this feed and detects the new article within a few hours. It automatically generates platform-specific posts and schedules the first post to go live 24–48 hours after publication. The remaining posts in the 12-month drip campaign are scheduled automatically from there.

**Step 8 — Medium Cross-Posting**  
One to two weeks after the article goes live on NexFortis, Hassan uses Medium's Import Tool to import the article. Medium automatically sets a canonical tag pointing to the original NexFortis URL. The article includes a footer note: "Originally published at nexfortis.com/blog/[slug]."

**Step 9 — Monitoring**  
Google Search Console reports on which keywords the article is ranking for, its average position, and click-through rate. Google Analytics reports on organic traffic, time on page, and bounce rate. Monthly, Hassan reviews these reports to identify articles that are gaining traction, those that need content refreshes, and topic gaps to fill next.

### 1.4 How Components Communicate

- **Frase → Sanity:** Frase's API pushes article data (body, title, meta description, SEO score) to Sanity's Content API using Sanity's write token. This is a direct HTTPS API call initiated by the integration layer (a lightweight automation script or n8n workflow).
- **Sanity → Next.js:** The Next.js app makes read-only HTTPS requests to Sanity's GROQ query API to fetch published blog posts, author profiles, and category data. This happens at build time (for static generation) and again on a timed revalidation interval (ISR).
- **Next.js → RSS Feed:** The Next.js app generates a dynamic RSS feed endpoint that returns an XML document listing all published articles. This URL is what Missinglettr/SocialBee polls.
- **Telegram Bot → Storage:** The Telegram bot interacts with users over the Telegram Bot API. Answers are stored in a simple database table or structured text file, keyed by topic. This storage is separate from Sanity (answers are Hassan's raw notes, not published content).
- **Telegram Bot → Frase:** Answers are passed to Frase either manually (Hassan copies them into the Frase document) or automatically via Frase's API (the bot pushes the answers as a "brief" or document context before drafting begins).

## Part 2: Sanity CMS Setup

### 2.1 What Sanity Is

Sanity is a headless content management system. "Headless" means that Sanity only handles storing and managing content — it has no built-in front-end or website. Instead, your Next.js application fetches content from Sanity's API and decides how to display it.

Sanity has two parts you interact with:
- **Sanity Studio:** A browser-based dashboard where Hassan writes, edits, and publishes content. It is fully customizable and can be embedded at a route like nexfortis.com/studio so Hassan can manage content from his own domain.
- **Sanity Content API (GROQ):** The API the Next.js app calls to retrieve published articles. GROQ is Sanity's own query language — similar to SQL but designed for JSON documents. The Next.js site sends GROQ queries to retrieve exactly the fields it needs for each page.

### 2.2 How Sanity and Next.js Connect

At a high level:

1. Sanity hosts your content in its cloud (on the free tier, up to 10 GB of assets and unlimited documents).
2. The Next.js app uses Sanity's official JavaScript client to make GROQ queries to the Sanity API.
3. During a Next.js build, the app fetches all published blog posts from Sanity, generates static HTML pages for each one (at /blog/[slug]), and caches them.
4. On a periodic interval (the ISR revalidation window, for example every 60 seconds), Next.js checks whether the content has changed. If it has — for example, because Hassan just published a new article in Sanity Studio — the page is regenerated in the background and the new version replaces the old cached one.
5. For the Sanity Studio to be accessible at nexfortis.com/studio, a special Next.js route handles the Studio's HTML and JavaScript assets. Sanity provides an official Next.js plugin for this embedded Studio configuration.

### 2.3 Setting Up Sanity's Free Tier

1. Go to sanity.io and create an account.
2. Create a new project. Give it the name "NexFortis." Select the free tier (no credit card required).
3. Sanity will assign a Project ID — a short alphanumeric string. This is needed in the Next.js app's configuration.
4. Choose "production" as the dataset name (Sanity uses "datasets" similar to database schemas).
5. In the Sanity project settings, generate two API tokens:
   - A **read-only token** for the Next.js front-end to fetch published content.
   - A **write token** for the automation layer (Frase integration) to push draft articles.
6. Add the NexFortis website domain (on Render) to the CORS origins in Sanity's API settings, so the Next.js app is allowed to make API calls.
7. Install the Sanity CLI on your development machine. Use it to initialize the Sanity Studio configuration inside the Next.js project repository.

### 2.4 Content Models

The three schemas below define the structure of all content in Sanity. Full field-by-field descriptions are in Part 11.

**Blog Post schema** is the core content type. It stores the article headline, body, metadata, SEO fields, publication status, and internal references to the author and categories.

**Author schema** stores Hassan's profile information — bio, credentials, headshot, and social links. There will likely only be one author record ("Hassan Sadiq"), but the schema is built generically to support future contributors.

**Category schema** stores topic categories like "Microsoft 365," "QuickBooks," and "Cybersecurity," each tagged with its parent pillar so the site can group articles by service line.

### 2.5 How Sanity Handles Images

Sanity has a built-in image asset pipeline called Sanity CDN. When you upload an image in Sanity Studio — for example, a featured image for a blog post — Sanity stores it and exposes it via a CDN URL. 

The key feature is that the CDN URL accepts transformation parameters. You can append width, height, quality, and format parameters to the URL to get a resized or WebP-converted version of the image on the fly, without storing multiple copies. The Next.js app uses this to serve appropriately sized images to desktop and mobile visitors.

When defining the featured image field in the Blog Post schema, always include an `alt` text field alongside the image field itself. Alt text is required for accessibility and SEO.

### 2.6 Preview Mode

Sanity supports draft preview. When Hassan is reviewing an article in "draft" status, the Next.js app can be configured to fetch draft (unpublished) content from Sanity using a special preview token. This lets Hassan see exactly how the article will look on the live site before approving it. Preview mode is activated by visiting a special preview URL and is deactivated when Hassan closes the preview session. Sanity's official Next.js package handles the preview session cookie logic.

### 2.7 Blog Post Page Rendering — ISR

Every blog post page at /blog/[slug] uses Incremental Static Regeneration (ISR). This means:

- At build time, Next.js pre-generates static HTML for every published article it finds in Sanity.
- Each static page has a revalidation interval configured (a value in seconds, for example 60). After this time window passes, Next.js will regenerate the page in the background the next time a visitor requests it.
- This gives you static site performance (fast page loads, no waiting for server rendering) while still allowing content updates to appear without a full site rebuild.
- For newly published articles, the ISR interval means the article may take up to 60 seconds (or whatever the revalidation window is set to) to appear live after Hassan clicks "publish" in Sanity Studio. This is acceptable for a content-focused site.

Alternatively, Sanity supports webhooks. When a document is published, Sanity can send a webhook to Render (where the Next.js app is hosted) to trigger an on-demand ISR revalidation. This means the new article appears live within seconds of publication, without waiting for the revalidation interval. This is the recommended approach for production.

## Part 3: Frase.io Integration

### 3.1 How Frase's Workflow Works

Frase is a content research and writing tool that analyzes the top 20 organic search results for a given keyword and extracts what topics, entities, headers, and questions those articles cover. This analysis tells you what Google already considers "comprehensive" content for that keyword.

The workflow inside Frase has four stages:

1. **Research:** Enter the target keyword. Frase fetches the top-ranking articles and builds a content brief showing all the topics they cover, how many words they are, and what questions they answer.
2. **Outline:** Build an article outline by selecting topics from the research brief. Frase shows which topic areas your outline already covers and which are missing.
3. **Draft:** Write the article inside Frase's editor, or use Frase's AI writing assistant to generate a draft based on the outline and the research. Hassan's interview answers (gathered by the Telegram bot) are pasted or pushed into the document at this stage as the source of original insights and examples.
4. **Optimize:** The Frase editor shows a real-time content score (0–100) that reflects how comprehensively the article covers the topics found in top-ranking competitor articles. The goal is to reach a score above 80 before the article is considered ready to publish.

Frase's Starter plan ($49/month as of early 2026) supports 4 articles per month, which matches the NexFortis publishing cadence.

### 3.2 How the Frase API Works

Frase exposes a REST API that allows programmatic access to your document library. The API lets you:
- List all documents in your Frase workspace
- Retrieve the content and SEO score of a specific document
- Create or update documents

For the NexFortis pipeline, the API is used in one direction: **Frase pushes finalized draft content into Sanity**. This is initiated by an automation script (or n8n workflow) that:

1. Detects that a Frase document has reached a score above 80 (either by polling or by manual trigger).
2. Calls the Frase API to retrieve the full document content, including body text, title, meta description, and SEO score.
3. Transforms the Frase document content into Sanity's document format (the portable text structure that Sanity uses for rich-text body fields).
4. Calls the Sanity Content API with the write token to create a new Blog Post document in Sanity with a status of "draft."

### 3.3 The Full Editorial Workflow

The complete editorial cycle, from Frase to live, is:

| Stage | Location | Status | Who Does It |
|---|---|---|---|
| Topic selected, brief created | Frase | In Progress | Hassan / Auto |
| Interview questions sent | Telegram Bot | Pending | Bot |
| Hassan answers questions | Telegram | Answered | Hassan |
| Answers pasted into Frase, draft written | Frase | Drafting | Hassan / AI |
| Content score reaches 80+ | Frase | Ready to Export | Hassan |
| Draft pushed to Sanity via API | Automation | Draft | Auto |
| Hassan reviews and edits in Sanity Studio | Sanity | Review | Hassan |
| Hassan approves, changes status | Sanity | Published | Hassan |
| Page goes live on NexFortis | Next.js | Live | Auto (ISR) |

### 3.4 What Data Frase Provides via API

When the integration layer calls the Frase API to retrieve a finished document, it can expect to receive:

- **Article title:** The headline Hassan finalized in Frase.
- **Body content:** The full article in HTML or plain text format. This needs to be converted to Sanity's portable text format before being written to Sanity.
- **SEO score:** The numeric content optimization score (0–100). This is stored in the `seoScore` field of the Sanity Blog Post document.
- **Target keyword:** The primary keyword the article was written for.
- **Recommended meta description:** Frase's suggested meta description based on the article content.

Word count, outline structure, and competitor references are available in the Frase UI but are not typically needed in the Sanity document.

### 3.5 Setting Up the Frase–Sanity Connection

The connection requires the following elements:

1. **A Frase API key:** Generated in the Frase account settings under API access.
2. **A Sanity write token:** Generated in the Sanity project settings (described in Part 2.3).
3. **A Frase-to-Sanity content transformer:** A piece of logic (written as an n8n workflow or a standalone script) that:
   - Accepts the raw Frase document as input
   - Strips HTML tags and converts the body content into Sanity's portable text format (an array of block objects)
   - Maps Frase fields to Sanity schema fields
   - Sets the `status` field to "draft"
   - Sets the `author` reference to Hassan's Author document ID in Sanity
   - Calls the Sanity API to create the document

The transformer should handle the following edge cases:
- Images in the Frase body (strip them out or convert to Sanity image references)
- Headings (map H2, H3 to the appropriate portable text block styles)
- Lists (map to Sanity's list block type)
- Hyperlinks (preserve as annotations in portable text)

## Part 4: Telegram Bot for Smart Interview Questions

### 4.1 What Hassan Experiences

From Hassan's perspective, every Monday morning he receives a Telegram message from the NexFortis Content Bot. The message starts by identifying the upcoming article topic, then presents 3–5 specific questions. These questions are not generic content questions. They are targeted directly at knowledge gaps in what already ranks on Google for that keyword.

Example (for a topic like "Microsoft 365 Business Premium vs. Standard for small businesses"):

> *Upcoming article topic: Microsoft 365 Business Premium vs. Standard — which plan is right for small businesses*
>
> *The top-ranking articles on this topic cover pricing and feature lists well, but none of them mention real-world enforcement gaps or conditional access policy complexity. I have 3 questions for you:*
>
> *1. Can you walk through a specific client situation where you recommended Premium over Standard — what threat or compliance requirement pushed the decision?*
>
> *2. What is the most common mistake you see when businesses try to set up Conditional Access without guidance?*
>
> *3. Have you ever seen Defender for Business (included in Premium) catch something a traditional antivirus missed? What happened?*

Hassan types his answers directly in Telegram, one message per question, or sends a voice note. The bot confirms receipt after each answer.

### 4.2 Question Generation Logic

The question generation process runs before each weekly send. It is not a static template — each batch of questions is unique to the topic and grounded in live SERP data. The process works as follows:

**Step 1 — SERP Analysis**  
The bot's backend (or a connected automation) queries a search API (such as the Google Custom Search API or a third-party SERP tool) for the target keyword. It retrieves the top 10–15 organic results and fetches the text content of those pages.

**Step 2 — Gap Detection**  
The content from the top-ranking pages is passed to a language model (such as the OpenAI API with GPT-4 or equivalent). The model is prompted to identify what is covered extensively versus what is only mentioned superficially or not mentioned at all. The output is a structured list of knowledge gaps — topics that readers would benefit from but that existing articles do not adequately address.

**Step 3 — Question Crafting**  
The identified gaps are turned into specific interview questions. The model is prompted to frame questions in a way that draws out Hassan's direct experience, specific examples, and professional judgment — not generic opinions. The questions should be:
- Grounded in a specific scenario or client situation
- Focused on extracting tacit knowledge (things Hassan knows that aren't written anywhere)
- Directly relevant to the gap identified

**Step 4 — Review and Send**  
The generated questions are stored in the bot's question queue, associated with the topic. On the scheduled send day, the bot sends them to Hassan's Telegram account.

**Variety requirement:** The same question must never appear twice across different topics. The generation logic uses the SERP gap analysis to ensure every batch is unique to that specific keyword and article. The language model is instructed to explicitly avoid generic content-writing advice questions like "What is your opinion on X?" or "How important is Y to small businesses?"

### 4.3 How Answers Are Processed

**Text answers:** When Hassan types a reply in Telegram, the bot captures the message text. Each answer is stored with a reference to the question it answers and the topic it belongs to. The storage can be as simple as a structured table in Supabase or Neon with columns for topic, question, answer, timestamp, and answer type (text or voice).

**Voice note answers:** When Hassan sends a voice note, the bot receives the audio file from Telegram's API. The audio file is sent to a transcription service — Whisper (OpenAI's audio transcription model) is the recommended choice, available via the OpenAI API at low cost. The transcribed text is stored the same way as a text answer. The original audio file can optionally be stored as well for reference.

**Confirmation message:** After each answer is received and stored, the bot replies with a brief confirmation: "Got it — answer 2 of 3 received." After all questions for a topic are answered, the bot sends a final summary message listing all the topic's questions and answers, and confirms that the content will be passed to Frase.

**Passing answers to Frase:** The collected answers for a topic are compiled into a structured document (a plain-text file or a formatted brief). This document is either:
- Manually pasted by Hassan into the Frase document for that topic, or
- Pushed automatically to Frase via the API, inserted as a "brief" section at the top of the Frase document before drafting begins.

### 4.4 Conversation Flow Summary

1. Bot sends topic name + 3–5 questions to Hassan via Telegram (Monday morning, scheduled).
2. Hassan reads the questions and replies over the course of the day — one answer per message, at his own pace.
3. Bot stores each answer with its question reference.
4. After all questions are answered, bot sends a confirmation summary.
5. Answers are compiled and passed to Frase as input context.
6. Frase draft is written using those answers as the source of original insight.

### 4.5 Scheduling

The bot uses a scheduled task (a cron job or time-based trigger in n8n) to send questions every Monday at a set time — for example, 8:00 AM Eastern time. This is early enough that Hassan can answer throughout the morning, and the answers are ready for the Frase drafting session later in the week.

The topic queue (the list of upcoming topics) is maintained manually. Hassan adds upcoming topics to a simple list (a Supabase table, a Notion database, or a plain text file), and the bot picks the next topic from the queue on each send day.

## Part 5: Social Media Distribution

### 5.1 How Missinglettr Works

Missinglettr is a social media automation tool designed specifically for blog content. It reads your blog's RSS feed and, when it detects a new article, automatically creates a 12-month campaign of social media posts based on that article's content.

Here is how it works in practice:

1. You connect Missinglettr to your blog RSS feed (the URL of the feed generated by the NexFortis Next.js app).
2. You connect your social media accounts: LinkedIn company page, Instagram, Facebook page, and X account.
3. When Missinglettr detects a new article in the RSS feed, it generates a set of post variants — short quotes, key points, questions, statistics — drawn from the article text.
4. It schedules these posts as a drip campaign: the first post goes out 24–48 hours after publication, and subsequent posts are spaced out monthly over 12 months.
5. You review and approve the campaign before it starts (Missinglettr shows you a preview of all generated posts).

The Missinglettr Solo plan starts at around $9/month and supports one workspace. This is sufficient for NexFortis.

### 5.2 How SocialBee Works (Alternative)

SocialBee is a broader social media scheduling tool. Unlike Missinglettr, which is focused on blog-driven campaigns, SocialBee also supports standalone content creation — posting tips, quotes, promotional content, and curated content that is not tied to a specific blog article.

If Hassan wants to maintain a social media presence that includes more than just article promotion (for example, weekly IT tips, industry news commentary, or Microsoft 365 quick tips), SocialBee is the better choice. SocialBee's Bootstrap plan starts at around $29/month.

The two tools are not mutually exclusive. Missinglettr handles the automated blog drip campaigns; SocialBee handles everything else. For the initial launch, start with Missinglettr only and add SocialBee later if there is a need for standalone social content.

### 5.3 Distribution Rules by Platform

Each platform receives a different version of the content. The same text must never be posted across all platforms simultaneously — this is poor engagement practice and reduces reach.

**LinkedIn**
- Format: A unique summary of the article, written as a standalone post. Not an excerpt — a reformatted summary with LinkedIn's native text formatting (line breaks, short paragraphs).
- Length: 200–400 words.
- Content: Opens with a hook relevant to business owners or IT managers. Shares 2–3 key takeaways from the article. Closes with a direct link to the full article on NexFortis.
- What to avoid: Do not post the full article text on LinkedIn. Do not use the same opening sentence as the article itself. LinkedIn suppresses posts with external links in the first line, so the link should appear at the end of the post body or in the first comment.
- Frequency: One post per article per month for the 12-month campaign. Total: 12 LinkedIn posts per article.

**X (Twitter)**
- Format: A thread.
- Structure: The first tweet in the thread is a hook or bold claim from the article. Each subsequent tweet covers one key point from the article. The final tweet in the thread includes the link to the full article and a call to action ("Full breakdown at the link below").
- Length: 4–7 tweets per thread. Each tweet stays under 280 characters.
- Content: Focus on the most surprising, counterintuitive, or practically useful points from the article. Do not simply summarize in order — select the most engagement-worthy points.
- Frequency: One thread per article per month for the campaign.

**Facebook**
- Format: A short excerpt from the article (2–3 sentences) followed by a link and the featured image.
- Length: Under 100 words in the post body.
- Content: Keep it conversational. The excerpt should be the most accessible or universally relatable part of the article.
- Image: Always include the article's featured image. Facebook gives higher organic reach to posts with images.
- Frequency: One post per article per month.

**Instagram**
- Format: Option A — a visual carousel where each slide presents one key takeaway from the article as a text graphic. Option B — a single image (the featured image, redesigned as a square or 4:5 image) with a key takeaway as the caption.
- Caption: 3–5 sentences maximum. Ends with "Link in bio" directing followers to the NexFortis website.
- Content: Instagram audiences respond to practical, numbered tips (e.g., "5 signs your small business needs Microsoft 365 Business Premium"). Format accordingly.
- Note: Instagram does not allow clickable links in post captions. The bio link should be updated to point to the most recent article or to a Linktree-style page listing recent articles.
- Frequency: One post per article per month.

### 5.4 Timing

Social distribution begins 24–48 hours after the blog post goes live on NexFortis. This delay serves two purposes: it gives search engines time to crawl and index the article before social traffic arrives, and it ensures the article is fully live and loading correctly before it is promoted.

Missinglettr handles this timing automatically once you set the delay preference in your campaign settings.

### 5.5 Connecting All Four Accounts

In Missinglettr (or SocialBee), navigate to the connected accounts section and authenticate each platform:

- **LinkedIn:** Authenticate via LinkedIn OAuth. You can connect a personal profile or a Company Page. For NexFortis, connect the Company Page.
- **X (Twitter):** Authenticate via X's OAuth. This connects the @NexFortis account directly.
- **Facebook:** Authenticate via Facebook OAuth. You will be asked to grant access to your Facebook Pages. Select the NexFortis Facebook Page.
- **Instagram:** Instagram must be connected as a Business or Creator account, and it must be linked to a Facebook Page. Once the Facebook Page is connected, Instagram can be added as an additional channel. Personal Instagram accounts cannot be connected to scheduling tools.

All four accounts were created in April 2026, so they are new accounts with no existing followers. The social campaign will build the audience gradually over time as the drip campaigns run.

## Part 6: Medium Cross-Posting

### 6.1 How Medium's Import Tool Works

Medium provides a built-in Import Tool that lets you import an article from any public URL. You give it the URL of the published NexFortis article, and Medium fetches the page content, strips the navigation and layout, and imports just the article body into a new Medium draft. You can then edit the draft before publishing.

The Import Tool is accessed from the Medium account's Stories section.

### 6.2 Canonical Tags and SEO

When you import an article using Medium's Import Tool, Medium automatically sets the canonical tag on the Medium version of the article to point back to the original URL (the NexFortis article). This is critical for SEO: it tells Google that the original version of the content is on NexFortis.com, not on Medium. Google does not penalize NexFortis for duplicate content because the canonical tag directs all ranking authority to the original.

This canonical tag is set automatically by Medium's Import Tool — you do not need to do anything manually to configure it.

### 6.3 Footer Note

Before publishing the Medium version, add the following note at the bottom of the article:

> *Originally published at nexfortis.com/blog/[slug] on [publication date].*

This makes the syndication transparent to readers and reinforces the NexFortis brand.

### 6.4 Timing

Wait 1–2 weeks after the article is published on NexFortis before importing to Medium. This waiting period gives Google time to crawl and index the original on NexFortis first. If you import to Medium on the same day as publication, there is a small risk that Google indexes the Medium version first, even with the canonical tag in place.

### 6.5 Manual vs. Automated

Initially, the Medium cross-posting step is done manually by Hassan every 1–2 weeks. Once the pipeline is running smoothly and there is a consistent publishing cadence, this can be automated using Medium's API, which supports creating and publishing posts programmatically. However, automation of this step is a Phase 4 task — there is no urgency to automate it at launch.

## Part 7: SEO Infrastructure

### 7.1 JSON-LD Structured Data

Every blog post page must include a JSON-LD structured data block in the HTML head. JSON-LD is a standard format that tells search engines exactly what the page is about in machine-readable terms. Google uses this data to create rich results (such as article cards, author panels, and knowledge graph entries).

The following schemas must be implemented:

**Article Schema (per blog post)**  
This tells Google that the page is a news or blog article. It must include:
- `headline`: The article's meta title (not necessarily the same as the displayed H1)
- `datePublished`: The ISO 8601 datetime the article first went live
- `dateModified`: The ISO 8601 datetime the article was last genuinely updated
- `author`: A reference to a Person schema (Hassan's profile), or the author's name and URL inline
- `publisher`: A reference to an Organization schema (NexFortis)
- `image`: The URL of the featured image
- `description`: The meta description
- `mainEntityOfPage`: The canonical URL of this article

**Person Schema (Hassan — site-wide)**  
This establishes Hassan as a named author entity recognized by Google. It must include:
- Full name
- Job title
- Profile URL (nexfortis.com/about)
- Social profile URLs (LinkedIn, X)
- A brief description of credentials and expertise

This schema can live on the About page and be referenced from each article, or it can be embedded on every article page.

**Organization Schema (NexFortis — site-wide)**  
This establishes NexFortis as a publisher entity. It must include:
- Organization name
- Website URL
- Logo URL
- Contact information

This schema lives in the site-wide layout (rendered on every page).

### 7.2 Open Graph and Twitter Card Meta Tags

Every blog post page must include Open Graph and Twitter Card meta tags in the HTML head. These tags control how the article appears when shared on social media.

Required Open Graph tags per blog post:
- `og:title` — the article's meta title
- `og:description` — the article's meta description
- `og:url` — the canonical URL
- `og:type` — set to "article"
- `og:image` — the featured image URL (optimized to 1200×630 pixels for best display across platforms)
- `og:image:alt` — alt text for the featured image
- `article:published_time` — the publication datetime
- `article:modified_time` — the last updated datetime
- `article:author` — Hassan's profile URL

Required Twitter Card tags per blog post:
- `twitter:card` — set to "summary_large_image"
- `twitter:title` — the article's meta title
- `twitter:description` — the article's meta description
- `twitter:image` — the featured image URL

These tags are generated dynamically by the Next.js app using the data fetched from Sanity for each blog post.

### 7.3 Sitemap.xml

The NexFortis website must have a sitemap.xml file that includes all published blog posts. This file tells search engine crawlers what pages exist on the site and when they were last updated.

The sitemap for blog posts must include:
- The URL of each published blog post (/blog/[slug])
- The `lastmod` date (the `lastUpdated` datetime from Sanity, or `publishedAt` if the article has never been updated)
- A change frequency hint (for blog posts, use "monthly" as a default)
- A priority value (blog posts: 0.8; homepage: 1.0)

The sitemap is generated dynamically in Next.js, not as a static file. It fetches all published post slugs and dates from Sanity and returns them as valid XML. The sitemap URL is submitted to Google Search Console once the site is live.

### 7.4 Canonical Tags

Every blog post page must include a self-referencing canonical tag in the HTML head. The canonical tag declares the authoritative version of the page's URL, preventing any issues from URL variations (with/without trailing slashes, query parameters, etc.).

The canonical URL format for all blog posts is: `https://nexfortis.com/blog/[slug]`

No trailing slash. Always HTTPS. Always the full absolute URL.

### 7.5 Blog Post URL Structure

All blog posts live at the path `/blog/[slug]`. The slug is auto-generated from the article title in Sanity (lowercase, hyphens replace spaces, special characters removed). Once a slug is set and the article is published, it must never change — changing a URL breaks existing inbound links and social shares.

Examples:
- /blog/quickbooks-online-vs-desktop-for-small-business
- /blog/microsoft-365-business-premium-vs-standard
- /blog/ransomware-protection-for-small-businesses-ontario

### 7.6 Internal Linking Strategy

Internal linking is critical for topical authority. Every article must be connected to the rest of the content cluster it belongs to. The rules are:

**Cluster articles** (specific, long-tail topic articles) must:
- Include at least one link to their parent **pillar page** (the main service page or comprehensive guide for that pillar)
- Include 2–3 links to other cluster articles within the same pillar

**Pillar pages** must:
- Include a section that lists and links to all cluster articles within that pillar
- These links should be kept up to date as new cluster articles are published

**Cross-pillar linking** is acceptable when there is a genuine topical connection (for example, an IT article about Microsoft 365 linking to a QuickBooks article about cloud accounting for businesses using Microsoft tools).

Internal links must use descriptive anchor text that describes what the linked page is about. Avoid generic anchors like "click here" or "read more."

## Part 8: Content Quality Checklist

### 8.1 Purpose

The quality checklist is a mandatory gate that every article must pass before its status can be changed to "published" in Sanity. It prevents low-quality articles from going live and enforces consistent editorial standards.

The checklist has two layers:

1. **Automated checks:** These can be verified programmatically within the Sanity publish workflow or by a pre-publish validation script. They check objective, measurable criteria.
2. **Manual checks:** These require Hassan's judgment and cannot be fully automated. They are displayed as a checklist in the Sanity Studio interface, which Hassan must explicitly confirm before publishing.

### 8.2 Automated Checks (system verifies these)

| Check | Criteria | What Fails |
|---|---|---|
| Frase content score | Must be 80 or higher out of 100 | Any article where `seoScore` field is below 80 or is empty |
| Author assigned | Must have a reference to Hassan's Author document | Empty author field |
| Publication date | Must have a `publishedAt` value | Empty or future-dated `publishedAt` |
| Featured image | Must have a featured image with alt text | No image, or image with empty alt text |
| Category assigned | Must have at least one category reference | Empty categories array |
| Pillar tag assigned | Must have a pillar value selected | Empty pillar field |
| Meta title length | Must be under 60 characters | Meta title exceeding 60 characters |
| Meta description length | Must be under 160 characters | Meta description exceeding 160 characters |
| Slug set | Must have a valid, non-empty slug | Empty slug |
| Monthly article count | Must not exceed 8 articles published in the current calendar month | If the 9th article attempts to publish in the same month, the system warns (does not block — Hassan makes the final call) |

### 8.3 Manual Checks (Hassan confirms these before publishing)

These are displayed in the Sanity Studio interface as a checklist that Hassan ticks off before approving. The article cannot move from "review" to "published" without all boxes checked:

- [ ] Article contains at least one real-world example or case study reference (not a hypothetical — a reference to a specific client scenario, industry situation, or documented real event)
- [ ] Article title does not use clickbait patterns (no excessive superlatives like "ultimate," "best ever," "you won't believe"; no titles structured purely as "X ways to..." or "X things every..." with a high number; straightforward informational titles only)
- [ ] Article includes 2–3 internal links to related NexFortis content
- [ ] Article includes 1–2 external links to authoritative sources (official vendor documentation, government sites, established industry publications — not general news sites or competitors)
- [ ] The content has been read from start to finish by Hassan and sounds like it was written by someone with real IT experience, not generic AI filler

### 8.4 Monthly Volume Cap

NexFortis publishes a maximum of 5–6 articles per month under steady-state operation. The system enforces a soft cap of 8 articles per month. Publishing more than 8 articles in a single month is a signal that quality may be suffering from volume pressure. The system warns Hassan at 7 articles published in a given month and again at 8. It never blocks publication — Hassan makes the final call.

## Part 9: Analytics & Monitoring

### 9.1 Google Search Console

Google Search Console (GSC) is free and tracks how NexFortis articles perform in Google's organic search results.

**Setup:** Add the NexFortis domain to GSC, verify ownership (via a DNS TXT record or HTML file upload on Render), and submit the sitemap.xml URL.

**What to monitor per article:**
- **Impressions:** How many times the article appeared in Google search results
- **Clicks:** How many times a user clicked through to the article
- **Average position:** Where the article ranks on average for its target keyword
- **Click-through rate (CTR):** Clicks ÷ impressions. A CTR below 2% for a top-10 ranking suggests the title or meta description needs improvement.

**Monthly review:** Each month, identify which articles gained the most impressions and which gained the most clicks. Compare average position against the previous month to see which articles are rising or falling in rankings. Export this data to a simple spreadsheet to track trends over time.

**Keyword Gap Analysis:** GSC also shows what queries (keywords) are triggering impressions but receiving few clicks. These are candidates for new articles or for updating existing articles to better address those queries.

### 9.2 Google Analytics

Google Analytics 4 (GA4) is free and tracks visitor behavior on the NexFortis site.

**Setup:** Add the GA4 measurement ID to the Next.js app. The recommended approach is to add the GA4 script via the Next.js Script component in the app's root layout, so it loads on every page. Submit the NexFortis property in GA4 and link it to the Google Search Console property for combined organic search + behavior data.

**What to monitor per article:**
- **Organic sessions:** How many sessions came from Google search for each article
- **Engagement rate:** The percentage of sessions where the visitor meaningfully engaged (scrolled, clicked, spent time) vs. immediately bounced
- **Average engagement time:** Time spent reading. A well-written article in a technical niche should see 3–5 minutes average engagement time. Under 1 minute suggests the content is not matching search intent.
- **Scroll depth:** (Enabled via GA4 enhanced measurement) How far visitors scroll. If most visitors leave at 30% scroll depth, the article's opening section may not be compelling enough to keep reading.

### 9.3 Social Media Analytics

**Missinglettr analytics** show:
- Which articles have the highest click-through rate from social posts
- Which platforms drive the most traffic back to NexFortis
- Which post formats (quote, question, statistic) get the most engagement per platform

**SocialBee analytics** (if used) provide similar metrics with more granular platform breakdowns.

Review social analytics monthly. If one platform consistently underperforms, adjust the content style for that platform in the drip campaign settings.

### 9.4 Monthly Content Review

On the first Monday of each month, conduct a 30-minute content performance review covering:

1. **Traffic review:** Pull the top 10 articles by organic sessions from GA4. Note which articles are growing and which are flat or declining.
2. **Ranking review:** Pull the top 20 queries by impressions from GSC. Identify any queries where NexFortis ranks on page 2 (positions 11–20). These are high-priority candidates for a content refresh, as they are close to the first page.
3. **Social review:** Check Missinglettr/SocialBee analytics for click-through data. Identify which articles are driving social traffic and which are not.
4. **Update candidates:** Flag any article that meets any of these criteria:
   - Published more than 6 months ago AND ranking is declining
   - Has new, materially important information that was not available at time of publication (e.g., a Microsoft product update, a CRA rule change)
   - The featured tools or product versions mentioned in the article have changed
5. **Next month's topics:** Based on keyword gap analysis from GSC, identify 2–3 new topic candidates for the next month's publishing schedule.

### 9.5 When to Refresh an Article

Genuine content refresh criteria (all of these require real new content, not cosmetic changes):

- The article references a product version, software feature, or regulatory rule that has materially changed
- A new, authoritative data source or study has been published that strengthens or contradicts a claim in the article
- The article is ranking between positions 5–15 for its target keyword and a focused expansion of thin sections could push it higher
- Reader feedback or comments have revealed a common question the article does not answer

**What is NOT a legitimate refresh:**
- Changing the `lastUpdated` date without changing any actual content
- Adding a new paragraph that repeats what is already in the article
- Replacing words with synonyms
- Adding unnecessary padding to increase word count

Only update the `lastUpdated` field in Sanity when genuine new content has been added or factually incorrect content has been corrected.

## Part 11: Database Schema

This section describes every field in each Sanity schema in plain language. No code — just the names, types, purposes, and constraints.

### 11.1 Blog Post Schema

This is the primary content type. Every published article on NexFortis is a Blog Post document.

| Field Name | Type | Required? | Constraints / Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| `title` | Plain text | Yes | The article headline displayed on the page. Also used as the default for the `metaTitle` if not explicitly set. No character limit here (the character limit is on `metaTitle`). |
| `slug` | Slug (auto-generated) | Yes | Generated automatically from `title` when the document is first created. Lowercase, words separated by hyphens, special characters removed. Example: "microsoft-365-business-premium-vs-standard". Once set and the article is published, this field must never be changed. |
| `body` | Rich text (portable text) | Yes | The full article body. Supports: H2 and H3 headings, paragraph text, bold, italic, hyperlinks, bulleted and numbered lists, blockquotes, inline images, and code blocks. Content is structured as a portable text array, which Next.js renders using the Sanity portable text renderer. |
| `excerpt` | Plain text | Yes | A short summary of the article — 1 to 3 sentences, maximum 300 characters. Used on blog index page cards, in Open Graph descriptions (if `metaDescription` is not set), and as a fallback for social sharing previews. Must not repeat the article's opening sentence verbatim. |
| `author` | Reference to Author document | Yes | A reference (foreign key) to Hassan's Author profile document. Every article must have exactly one author. |
| `publishedAt` | Datetime | Yes | The exact date and time the article was first made live. Set automatically when the status is first changed to "published." Once set, this field should never be changed. Displayed publicly on the article page. |
| `lastUpdated` | Datetime | No | The date and time of the last genuine content update. Only populate this when real new information has been added or factually incorrect content has been corrected. Do not update this field for formatting changes, typo fixes, or cosmetic edits. When set, the Article JSON-LD schema uses this as `dateModified`. |
| `categories` | Array of references to Category documents | Yes | At least one category must be assigned. Multiple categories are allowed. |
| `pillar` | Dropdown (string enum) | Yes | Which service pillar this article belongs to. Options: "quickbooks", "managed-it-m365", "cybersecurity". Determines which pillar page the article links back to and how it is organized in the site's content architecture. |
| `clusterParent` | Reference to another Blog Post document | No | If this is a cluster article (a specific subtopic article), this field references its parent pillar page. Leave empty for pillar pages themselves. Used to build the internal linking structure: cluster articles link to their pillar parent. |
| `metaTitle` | Plain text | Yes | The SEO title tag — the text that appears in the browser tab and in Google search results. Maximum 60 characters. Should include the primary keyword. Does not need to be identical to `title`. Sanity Studio enforces the 60-character limit with a validation warning. |
| `metaDescription` | Plain text | Yes | The SEO meta description tag — the text that appears below the title in Google search results. Maximum 160 characters. Should be a compelling, keyword-inclusive sentence that describes what the reader will learn. Sanity Studio enforces the 160-character limit. |
| `featuredImage` | Image with alt text | Yes | The hero image displayed at the top of the article page and in social sharing previews. Stored in Sanity's image asset pipeline. The alt text sub-field is required — it must describe the image for accessibility and SEO. Recommended dimensions: at least 1200×630 pixels for social sharing compatibility. |
| `seoScore` | Number | No | The content optimization score from Frase (0–100). Populated automatically when the Frase-to-Sanity integration pushes the draft. A score below 80 will trigger a validation warning in Sanity Studio when the article attempts to move to "published." |
| `status` | Dropdown (string enum) | Yes | The editorial status of the article. Options: "draft" (just created or pushed from Frase, not ready for review), "review" (Hassan is reviewing it), "published" (live on the site), "archived" (no longer active but retained for reference). The Next.js app only queries for articles where `status = "published"`. |
| `interviewAnswers` | Plain text | No | Hassan's raw answers from the Telegram bot interview session for this article. This is a private field — it is not queried or displayed by the Next.js front-end. It lives in Sanity purely as a reference, so Hassan or a future editor can see the original thinking behind the article. The Sanity Studio UI should mark this field as hidden in the default view, visible only when explicitly expanded. |
| `socialDistributed` | Boolean (checkbox) | No | Defaults to false. When Missinglettr/SocialBee has successfully created and started the social campaign for this article, this field is manually checked to true. Serves as a record-keeping flag to avoid accidentally creating duplicate campaigns. |
